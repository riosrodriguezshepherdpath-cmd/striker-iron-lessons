Friction: The Hidden Force
‚è±Ô∏è 4 minutes
Friction is a force that slows things down when surfaces rub together.
Different surfaces create different amounts of friction. Rough surfaces (like grass) create more friction than smooth surfaces (like ice).
üîë KEY POINT: More friction = Objects slow down faster
That's why a ball rolls farther on a smooth gym floor than on bumpy grass. Less friction means the ball keeps its motion longer.
Soccer cleats are designed to CREATE friction so you don't slip when you change direction!

Momentum on the Field
‚è±Ô∏è 5 minutes
When you're running at full speed, you have momentum. Momentum is mass times velocity (how fast you're going).
The faster you run, the more momentum you have. The more momentum you have, the harder it is to stop.
üîë KEY POINT: Momentum = Mass √ó Speed
This is why when you're sprinting full speed, you can't just instantly stop. Your body has momentum and needs force (from your leg muscles pushing against the ground) to slow down.
Real game situation: A defender running toward you has momentum. You can use that momentum against them by faking one direction (they can't stop fast) and going the other way!

Balanced Forces
‚è±Ô∏è 4 minutes
Sometimes multiple forces act on an object at the same time.
If the forces are balanced (equal and opposite), the object doesn't change its motion. This is still Newton's First Law at work!
üîë KEY POINT: Balanced forces = No change in motion
Example: You're standing still on the field. Gravity pulls you down, but the ground pushes you up with equal force. These balanced forces mean you don't move.
Another example: You push a ball forward with 10 units of force, but wind resistance pushes back with 10 units of force. The ball doesn't speed up or slow down‚Äîit keeps constant speed.
