üìä T1 vs T2 Weighting

Core Concept:

T1-Weighted: Short TR + Short TE ‚Üí Fat BRIGHT, Water/CSF DARK

T2-Weighted: Long TR + Long TE ‚Üí Fat intermediate, Water/CSF BRIGHT

Direct Ask
What parameters create T1-weighted images?
Answer: Short TR and Short TE
This is how study guides present it - straightforward.
Reverse Logic
If you want to minimize T2 effects and maximize T1 contrast, what timing parameters should you use?
Answer: Short TR and Short TE
Same concept, different wording. "Minimize T2 effects" = use short TE before T2 decay occurs.
Scenario-Based
A technologist sets TR=500ms and TE=15ms. On the resulting images, subcutaneous fat appears bright and CSF appears dark. What type of weighting has been created?
Answer: T1-weighted
They're describing the RESULTS (fat bright, CSF dark) instead of asking directly. You need to recognize the pattern.
Problem-Solving
Your images show poor gray-white matter differentiation. The TR is 2500ms and TE is 80ms. To improve anatomical detail, what should you change?
Answer: Decrease BOTH TR and TE to create T1-weighting
They're asking you to recognize that T2 parameters (long TR/TE) don't show anatomy well. You need T1 weighting for anatomy.
Comparison
Which sequence parameters would produce the greatest signal difference between gray matter (T1=920ms) and white matter (T1=780ms)?
Answer: Short TR, Short TE (T1-weighting)
They're testing if you know that T1 differences are best shown with T1-weighting. They gave you the actual T1 values to make it look harder.
‚ö° SAR (Specific Absorption Rate)

Core Concept:

SAR = RF energy absorbed by tissue (W/kg)

FDA Limits: Whole body 4 W/kg, Head 3.2 W/kg, Extremity 12 W/kg per gram

Increases with: Higher field strength, faster sequences, larger flip angles

Direct Ask
What is the FDA whole-body SAR limit averaged over 15 minutes?
Answer: 4 W/kg
Straightforward memorization question.
Clinical Scenario
During a spine exam, the scanner displays a warning that SAR has exceeded safe levels. The patient reports feeling warm. What is the primary safety concern?
Answer: Excessive tissue heating/thermal injury
They describe the situation instead of asking "what does SAR measure?" You need to know that SAR = heating risk.
Problem-Solving
You need to reduce SAR in a fast spin echo sequence. Which modification would be MOST effective? A) Decrease TR B) Decrease flip angle C) Increase bandwidth D) Decrease matrix
Answer: B - Decrease flip angle
You need to know what CAUSES high SAR (flip angle, fast sequences, high field) to know how to reduce it.
Comparison
A patient needs both a 1.5T and 3T scan. How will SAR differ at 3T compared to 1.5T?
Answer: SAR is approximately 4x higher at 3T (increases with square of field strength)
Testing if you understand the RELATIONSHIP between field strength and SAR, not just memorizing a number.
üíâ Gadolinium Contrast Safety

Core Concept:

GFR <30 mL/min/1.73m¬≤ = HIGH RISK for NSF (Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis)

Half-life: ~1.5 hours (90 min) with normal renal function

Dose: 0.1 mmol/kg standard

Direct Ask
What GFR level indicates high risk for nephrogenic systemic fibrosis?
Answer: <30 mL/min/1.73m¬≤
Basic fact recall.
Clinical Decision
A 65-year-old patient's lab results show creatinine of 3.5 mg/dL and GFR of 25 mL/min/1.73m¬≤. The radiologist wants contrast-enhanced MRI. What should you do?
Answer: Alert the radiologist about NSF risk and discuss risk/benefit
They're testing if you recognize GFR <30 = danger, even when they also give you creatinine (distractor). You need to ACT on your knowledge.
Mechanism Understanding
Why does impaired renal function increase the risk of NSF with gadolinium administration?
Answer: Kidneys can't eliminate gadolinium efficiently, leading to tissue deposition
Testing if you understand WHY, not just memorize the rule. Understanding = retention.
Time-Based
A patient with normal kidney function receives gadolinium at 9:00 AM. At what time will approximately 75% of the contrast be eliminated from their body?
Answer: 12:00 PM (3 hours = 2 half-lives, ~75% eliminated)
Same fact (1.5 hour half-life) but asked mathematically. You need to APPLY the concept.
üìê K-Space Concepts

Core Concept:

K-space center: Low spatial frequencies = IMAGE CONTRAST

K-space periphery: High spatial frequencies = EDGE DETAIL/RESOLUTION

Each phase encode line = one line of k-space

Direct Ask
What information is stored in the center of k-space?
Answer: Image contrast and brightness (low spatial frequencies)
Textbook question.
Problem Identification
Your images have good spatial resolution but poor contrast. Which portion of k-space likely had data corruption?
Answer: Center of k-space
Reverse engineering: you need to know center = contrast to diagnose the problem.
Technical Understanding
If motion occurs while filling the peripheral lines of k-space, what image quality problem will result?
Answer: Blurred edges, loss of fine detail
Testing if you understand periphery = detail. Motion during periphery acquisition = blurry edges.
Technique Application
In a keyhole imaging technique, only the center of k-space is updated between dynamic scans. What imaging property is prioritized?
Answer: Temporal resolution (speed) while maintaining contrast
Advanced application: updating only center = fast scans with good contrast but sacrificing some resolution.
