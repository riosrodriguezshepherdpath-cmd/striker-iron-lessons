Week 1 Day 1: The Engine - Newton's 1st Law

COACH'S SCRIPT:

Alright striker, welcome to The Engine. This is where we learn how the world actually works. Today we're talking about Newton's First Law of Motion, and here's why it matters to you. Everything on that field follows rules. The ball, your body, your opponent. If you understand the rules, you control the game. Newton figured out that objects don't move unless you make them move, and they don't stop unless you make them stop. That means YOU are the force that changes everything. You want to win? You've got to understand how motion works. Let's break it down like a championship play. Pay attention, take notes, and let's get to work!
Phase 1: Objects at Rest
A soccer ball sitting on the grass will never move by itself, no matter how long you wait. This is the first part of Newton's First Law: an object at rest stays at rest unless a force acts on it, like your foot kicking the ball.
‚ùì QUESTION: If you put a soccer ball on the field and nobody touches it, what will happen to it?
Phase 2: Objects in Motion
Once you kick that ball and it starts rolling, it wants to keep rolling forever. But it doesn't, because friction from the grass and air resistance slow it down and eventually stop it, which is the second part of Newton's First Law: objects in motion stay in motion unless a force stops them.
‚ùì QUESTION: Why does a soccer ball eventually stop rolling on the field even if nobody touches it?
Phase 3: Force Changes Motion
When you're running down the field at full speed, you can't just instantly stop. You have to push against the ground with your feet to create a force that slows you down, and the faster you're moving, the more force you need to stop.
‚ùì QUESTION: Why is it harder to stop when you're running really fast compared to when you're jogging slowly?
Phase 4: Inertia in Action
Inertia is the fancy word for an object's resistance to changing its motion. A heavy medicine ball has more inertia than a regular soccer ball, which means it takes more force to get it moving and more force to stop it once it's moving.
‚ùì QUESTION: Which would be easier to kick across the field: a soccer ball or a bowling ball? Why?
Phase 5: Real World Examples
Newton's First Law is everywhere in your life, not just on the soccer field. When a car stops suddenly and you lurch forward, that's because your body wants to keep moving at the same speed the car was going, which is why we wear seatbelts to provide the stopping force.
‚ùì QUESTION: Name one example from your daily life where you can see Newton's First Law in action.
