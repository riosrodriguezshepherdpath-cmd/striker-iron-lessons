FEDERALIST STRUCTURE

FEDERALISM DEFINED
Constitutional division of power between government levels.
Creates overlapping yet distinct jurisdictions.
Prevents power concentration while enabling responsive governance.
FEDERAL LEVEL
Source: Article I Section 8 (enumerated powers)
Powers:
• National defense
• Interstate commerce regulation
• Currency
• Immigration
• Foreign policy
Supremacy Clause: Federal law > State law in federal jurisdiction
STATE LEVEL (ARIZONA)
Source: Tenth Amendment (reserved powers)
Powers:
• Education policy
• Intrastate commerce
• Criminal law
• Property law
• Election administration
• Public health regulation
LOCAL LEVEL (MUNICIPAL)
Source: Delegated from state (municipal charters)
Powers:
• Zoning
• Local taxation
• Public works
• Emergency services
• Parks and recreation
Local governments are NOT sovereign. They operate as administrative subdivisions of the state.
MAYORAL AUTHORITY
Strong-Mayor: Concentrated executive power, budget control, appointments.
Weak-Mayor: Ceremonial role, council-distributed authority.
Institutional design shapes governance capacity.
GUBERNATORIAL POWERS
• Sign/veto legislation
• Issue executive orders
• Appoint agency directors
• Command National Guard
• Grant pardons/commutations
• Convene special legislative sessions
STATE PREEMPTION DOCTRINE
State law supersedes conflicting municipal ordinances on statewide matters.
Types:
• Express preemption (explicit statutory prohibition)
• Implied preemption (comprehensive state scheme)
• Conflict preemption (impossible to comply with both)
SUBSIDIARITY PRINCIPLE
Decisions should be made at lowest competent level.
Local when local knowledge advantageous.
State/Federal when coordination or standardization needed.
Example: Trash collection (local) vs Highway system (state)
Understanding jurisdictional boundaries is essential for policy analysis and civic engagement.
