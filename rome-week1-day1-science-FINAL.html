Inertia: Objects At Rest
‚è± 6 MINUTES
Newton's 1st Law, Part 1: Objects at rest remain at rest.
Stationary objects maintain stasis absent external force application.
Inertia is resistance to motion change. Objects resist movement from rest.
TACTICAL EXAMPLE: Unmanned ground vehicle (UGV) in standby mode. No propulsion active. Vehicle remains stationary. Requires force input to initiate motion. This demonstrates inertial resistance.
Why this matters: Motion requires force. You cannot achieve kinetic change without force application.
Force types: Push, pull, thrust, impact. All overcome inertial resistance.

Inertia: Objects In Motion
‚è± 6 MINUTES
Newton's 1st Law, Part 2: Objects in motion remain in motion.
Moving objects maintain velocity absent external force application.
Velocity = speed + direction. Objects maintain both without force interference.
PROJECTILE EXAMPLE: Artillery round exits barrel. Initial velocity: 800 m/s. In vacuum (no air resistance), projectile maintains 800 m/s indefinitely. On Earth, air resistance and gravity alter trajectory. These are forces.
Key insight: Motion continuation is default state. Stopping requires force application.
Forces that stop motion: Friction, air resistance, impact, braking systems.

Mass and Inertial Magnitude
‚è± 6 MINUTES
Mass quantifies inertia. Greater mass = greater inertial resistance.
Force required to change motion scales with mass. Heavier objects resist more.
COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS: Light infantry carrier vs. main battle tank. Both stationary. Infantry carrier: 10 tons. Tank: 60 tons. Tank requires 6x more force to achieve same acceleration. This is mass-inertia relationship.
Tactical implication: High-mass systems trade maneuverability for durability. Low-mass systems prioritize agility.
This principle applies universally. From equipment selection to vehicle deployment.
