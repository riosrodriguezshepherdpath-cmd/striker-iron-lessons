COMMANDER'S BRIEFING: Officer, Arizona operates under a federalist system where power is divided between state and local governments, with cities governed by mayors and councils while the state is governed by a governor and legislature. This division exists not by accident but by strategic design: local governments can respond quickly to community-specific needs while state governments manage broader regional interests and coordinate resources across multiple jurisdictions. Understanding jurisdictional authority and the division of governmental powers is foundational to comprehending how policy is executed, how resources are allocated, and how citizens interface with governmental systems. Today's mission focuses on structural analysis of governmental organization. Master the hierarchy. Understand the authority chains. Execute.
PHASE 1: FEDERALISM AS STRUCTURAL DESIGN
Federalism is the constitutional division of power between different levels of government, creating overlapping yet distinct jurisdictions that prevent excessive power concentration while enabling responsive governance. Arizona's system exemplifies vertical federalism, where municipal corporations (cities) possess delegated authority from the state, which itself possesses reserved powers under the Tenth Amendment, creating a hierarchical structure where each level has defined constitutional responsibilities and limitations.
FEDERAL LEVEL: Constitutional powers enumerated in Article I Section 8: national defense, interstate commerce regulation, currency, immigration, foreign policy. Supremacy Clause establishes federal law precedence over state law in areas of federal jurisdiction. STATE LEVEL: Reserved powers under Tenth Amendment: education policy, intrastate commerce, criminal law, property law, election administration, public health regulation. Arizona Constitution establishes executive, legislative, and judicial branches at state level. LOCAL LEVEL: Delegated authority from state through municipal charters: zoning, local taxation, public works, emergency services, parks and recreation. Operates as administrative subdivision of state government rather than sovereign entity.

PHASE 2: MUNICIPAL EXECUTIVE AUTHORITY
Municipal mayors serve as chief executives of incorporated cities, wielding authority derived from city charters approved by voters and constrained by state enabling legislation. Mayoral power varies significantly based on governmental structure: strong-mayor systems concentrate executive authority in the mayor's office with independent budgetary and appointment powers, while weak-mayor systems distribute authority through city councils with the mayor serving primarily ceremonial functions, demonstrating how institutional design shapes effective governance capacity.

PHASE 4: JURISDICTIONAL BOUNDARIES AND PREEMPTION
State preemption doctrine establishes that state law supersedes conflicting municipal ordinances on matters of statewide concern, preventing jurisdictional fragmentation and ensuring policy consistency across Arizona. This doctrine operates through express preemption where state statutes explicitly prohibit local regulation, implied preemption where comprehensive state regulatory schemes occupy the field, and conflict preemption where local ordinances make state law compliance impossible, demonstrating how hierarchical authority resolves jurisdictional disputes in federalist systems.

PHASE 5: SUBSIDIARITY AND OPTIMAL GOVERNANCE LEVELS
Subsidiarity principle posits that governmental functions should be performed at the lowest competent level, allocating decisions to local authorities when local knowledge provides advantages and elevating to state or federal levels only when coordination, standardization, or resource pooling creates efficiencies. This principle explains why trash collection remains municipal while highway systems are state-managed and national defense is federal, demonstrating how optimal jurisdictional assignment balances localized responsiveness against economies of scale and coordination benefits.
